    Rights & Permissions

    Clear guidance for permissions, translations, course use, and rights enquiries.

    Strong presses make it easy for instructors, authors, editors, publishers, and researchers to understand how to request reuse rights. This page gives Babcock University Press that standard professional layer.

    Babcock University Press / Rights & Permissions

    Typical request types

    • Reusing text, tables, figures, or illustrations from a BU Press title.
    • Requesting translation, territorial, or adaptation rights.
    • Seeking permission for course packs, institutional learning use, or republication.
    Rights Scope

    What this page is designed to help with

    Rights and permissions are a standard public function of serious presses. Even where individual agreements differ, the request path and required information should be visible.

    Text and excerpt permissions

    Requests to quote, reprint, or reproduce portions of a BU Press title in another publication, report, collection, or editorial project.

    Image, figure, and table reuse

    Requests involving diagrams, charts, photographs, figures, or other graphic content appearing in a published work.

    Translation and territorial rights

    Requests from other publishers, institutions, or rights partners seeking language or regional rights tied to a BU Press title.

    What to Include

    Information that should accompany a rights request

    • Your full name, organization, and the best contact email for follow-up.
    • The title, author, and exact material you want to reuse or license.
    • The format of reuse: print, digital, course pack, translation, anthology, website, or other.
    • The territory, language, publication date, print run, and expected selling price if relevant.
    • Whether the material will be commercial, academic, institutional, internal, or open access in nature.

    Important rights note

    A press can only grant permissions for rights it actually holds. If a title contains third-party images, figures, or licensed source material, additional permission may still be required from the original rights holder.

    Rights clearance is strongest when requesters identify the exact material and intended use upfront.
    Common Cases

    Typical rights and permissions scenarios

    This gives Babcock a more standard public-facing rights posture, similar to what established presses present for authors, educators, and publishing partners.

    Course use and teaching packs

    Instructors requesting excerpts for teaching should state the exact material, course context, estimated audience, and whether distribution is print, digital, or restricted platform access.

    Translation enquiries

    Translation requests should identify language, territory, proposed publisher, publication timeline, and whether the request covers exclusive or non-exclusive rights.

    Excerpt republication

    Journals, magazines, and edited collections should identify the excerpt length, title context, intended print run, selling price, and publication type.

    Author and contract context

    Rights decisions may depend on the publishing agreement already in place for the work, especially around territorial, digital, translation, and adaptation terms.

    Rights Enquiries

    Contact the publishing office with a complete request

    The most professional rights workflow is a clear one. Send a detailed request to the publishing office so the team can confirm whether the press holds the rights and what additional information may be needed.
